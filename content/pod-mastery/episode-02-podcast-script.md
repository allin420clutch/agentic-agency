## SEGMENT 1: Start With Communities, Not Designs [1:30-4:30]

**HOST:** The first thing I want you to understand is this: **POD products sell because they make people feel something.** They signal identity. They solve problems. They give归属感.

A "Cat Mom" doesn't just want a cute t-shirt — they want the world to know they're part of a club they love. A "Night Shift Nurse" doesn't just need a coffee mug — they want recognition for work nobody sees.

So when you're researching niches, start by finding **communities**. Ask yourself:

- Where do specific groups of people gather online?
- What words and phrases do they use to describe themselves?
- What frustrates them that a product could fix?
- What do they proudly identify with that a product could announce?

You want communities because communities have **language.** And language is what you print on products.

If you've ever wondered why "Live Love Laugh" sells in one niche but "But first, coffee" sells in another — it's because each phrase speaks directly to a specific community's identity. You're not selling a shirt. You're selling a membership card.

[MUSIC BRIEFLY UNDER]

So your homework before you design anything: spend 30 minutes finding two or three communities relevant to your interests. Browse a Facebook group or subreddit. Read what people are posting. Note the words they use to describe themselves.

**That research becomes your design brief.**

## SEGMENT 2: Free Tools for Validating Product Ideas [4:30-8:00]

**HOST:** Alright, now let's talk tools. And I mean free tools — because we're not spending money on research. That's not how you build a sustainable business.

**Google Trends** is where I start. Just go to trends.google.com and search your niche keyword.

Here's what you're looking for: a five-year trend line that is steady or climbing. You're not looking for a viral spike — those crash. You're looking for consistent, ongoing interest.

[PAUSE]

I'll give you a real example. Let's say you're interested in the "Nurse Life" niche. You search "nurse gifts" on Google Trends and you see that line steadily rising over five years with predictable spikes every May around Nurse Appreciation Week.

That's a green light. That's an audience with sustained interest you can sell to year after year.

Now Etsy's bestseller pages. Go to Etsy, search your keyword, filter to best-selling items. Look for products with hundreds of reviews — those are proven sellers. They're proof that real people opened their wallets.

And don't just look at what sells — look at the design style and the price point. Are sellers charging $20-plus for a coffee mug? That's the margin you should target. If everyone's at $10, that niche might be race-to-the-bottom territory.

Pinterest is the third tool. Now here's the thing about Pinterest — it's not just a pretty image board. It's a search engine. People go there looking for ideas and inspiration.

So when you type a keyword into Pinterest's search bar, look at the autocomplete suggestions. Those suggestions are real queries real people are making. And if you click through, Pinterest will show you how many pins exist for each topic.

More pins means more interest. But more pins also means more competition. So look for niches where there's enough interest to validate demand, but not so much competition that differentiation is impossible.

Finally — and this one's underrated — **Exploding Topics.** It's a free tool that surfaces emerging trends before they go mainstream. You can search your general category and see topics that have rising interest over the past 6 to 12 months.

Getting in early on a rising trend can give you a serious head start. But you still want to validate using the other tools — because not every rising trend has staying power.

[PAUSE]

Four free tools: Google Trends, Etsy, Pinterest, and Exploding Topics. Use all four before you commit time to any niche.

## SEGMENT 3: The 3-Question Test [8:00-10:30]

**HOST:** I want to introduce you to something I call the **3-Question Test.** I use this every single time I'm evaluating a new niche before I invest time designing for it.

It's simple. Ask yourself:

**One — Is there a real community, not just a passing trend?**

Can you name active Facebook groups, subreddits, forums where this audience gathers? I'm talking groups with thousands of members, years of posts, consistent conversations. A passing trend has one moment in the sun and then silence. A real community keeps showing up.

**Two — Can I name five specific products this audience would actually buy?**

Not vague ideas — actual product types. A coffee mug. A t-shirt. A tote bag. A sticker. A pillow. If you can't quickly list five products, you don't have a product line. You have a mood.

**Three — Can I write phrases this audience would proudly display?**

POD products sell through words as much as art. Can you imagine at least three phrases this group would want on a t-shirt or a mug? Catchy sayings, inside jokes, proud declarations. If you can, you've got a design direction. If you can't, this niche might not be right for POD.

[MUSIC UNDER]

All three questions pass? This niche is worth pursuing.

One or two questions fail? That's a signal to either tighten up your niche definition or find a different one. You want all three green before you invest serious design time.

This test has saved me countless hours. I hope it helps you too.

## SEGMENT 4: Red Flags and Green Lights [10:30-12:30]

**HOST:** Before we wrap, let me give you a quick cheat sheet on evaluating niches.

**Red flags — things that tell me to step back:**

- Every design in the niche looks identical — same clipart, same fonts
- Prices have collapsed to $8-10 across the board — margin is gone
- No room for a unique angle because one style dominates completely
- The audience can't articulate what makes their group special — no identity to design toward

**Green lights — things that tell me this niche has potential:**

- The audience has inside jokes, special titles, or shared terminology
- Products are emotional — they make people feel seen and recognized
- You can find three or more active communities where they gather
- There's a predictable seasonal spike you can plan inventory around

Here's the bottom line: competitive niches aren't automatically bad. But if you're going to compete in a crowded space, you need a very specific angle. The research process helps you find that angle.

If you skip the research and just design what you think is cool, you're gambling. If you do the research first, you're building a business.
