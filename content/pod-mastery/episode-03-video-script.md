**[SCREEN: Show split comparison — a cluttered busy design vs. a clean bold design, both on t-shirt mockups]**

Here's the uncomfortable truth about print-on-demand.

Your design IS your product. There's no shelf placement. There's no store atmosphere. There's no salesperson explaining why your product is special. There's just... a screenshot. A mockup. A thumbnail.

**[SCREEN: Animate scrolling through Etsy search results at 2x speed]**

Look at how people actually browse. They're flying through thousands of listings. They stop on things that catch their eye for maybe one second. That's your window.

The best POD designs do one thing brilliantly: **they communicate instantly.** You see the design, you get it, you either want it or you don't. Done.

A bad design doesn't have to be ugly. It just has to be confusing. Too many elements. Too similar colors. Text that's too small. A focal point that's... unclear.

**[SCREEN: Side-by-side of "good" design — bold, simple, high contrast — and "bad" design — busy, low contrast, cluttered]**

*Bad design: A complex watercolor landscape with three different font styles, multiple icon elements, and a border pattern. It's not ugly — it's just impossible to process in a glance.*

The design principle that changes everything for POD: **design for thumbnail, then scale up.** If it works at 200 pixels, it will sing at full size. If it's hard to read at thumbnail, it's dead in the water.

## [DESIGN PRINCIPLES — 2:00–3:45]

**[SCREEN: Large animated text with icons for each principle]**

Let's break down the four non-negotiable POD design principles.

### Principle #1 — Simplicity

**[SCREEN: Animation showing a busy design simplifying down to a single element]**

Fewer elements. One clear focal point. You're not creating art for a gallery — you're creating a purchase trigger for a stranger's brain.

If your design has more than 2–3 distinct visual elements, you probably need to cut it down.

### Principle #2 — Bold Colors

**[SCREEN: Same design shown in muted tones vs. high-contrast vibrant version]**

High contrast wins on screens, in feeds, and in print. Think black shirt, bright white, one accent color. The contrast does the heavy lifting. It's what makes your design *pop* off the page.

### Principle #3 — White Space

**[SCREEN: A design with elements spread to the edges vs. the same design with centered elements and breathing room]**

White space isn't empty space — it's focus space. It directs the eye. It makes your central element more powerful. The best-selling POD designs are often surprisingly minimal.

### Principle #4 — Readable at Thumbnail Size

**[SCREEN: Zooming in on a search result thumbnail, then expanding the design to full size]**

This is where most people fail. Design your elements chunky and bold. If you have to squint to make out what it is, shrink it more. If you can't read the text clearly when the design is 3 inches wide on your monitor, that's your text going from bad to invisible on a t-shirt.

## [COMMON MISTAKES — 3:45–5:00]

**[SCREEN: Grid of 4–6 mistake examples with visual annotations]**

Okay, let's talk about the mistakes that are quietly killing your sales right now.

**Mistake #1 — Too Many Elements**
Busy designs = visual noise. Even if it's technically good art, it won't sell on a t-shirt. Simplify or cut.

**Mistake #2 — Low Contrast**
A gray design on a gray shirt is a ghost. Use your contrast. Make your design fight for attention.

**Mistake #3 — Tiny Text**
Design software makes everything look fine at 100% zoom. Print it at actual size and you'll see text that belongs on a birthday card written by a 6-year-old. Keep text bold and large.

**Mistake #4 — Ignoring Product Color**
Designing on a white canvas and then listing on a navy shirt? Those colors may not work together. **Always mock up on the actual product color you plan to sell.**

**Mistake #5 — Using Low-Res Images**
72 DPI images look fine on screen. They look blurry and unprofessional when printed. Always start with 300 DPI minimum, preferably 600.

## [WHERE TO GET DESIGNS — 5:00–6:00]

**[SCREEN: Split screen showing Fiverr, Upwork, Creative Market, and AI tool interfaces]**

So where do you actually get designs?

**Option 1 — Freelance**
Fiverr is great for quick-turnaround, budget-friendly custom designs. $15 to $75 typically gets you something solid. Upwork is better for ongoing relationships and higher-complexity work.

When commissioning: specify POD file requirements upfront. Demand AI or EPS files, not just PNGs. Give a clear creative brief.

**Option 2 — AI Tools**
Midjourney, Ideogram, DALL-E — these are useful for generating concepts, textures, and reference ideas. But here's the catch: AI outputs need significant artistic refinement before they're POD-ready. And the legal gray area is real — platforms and copyright law are still catching up.

Use AI as a tool, not a crutch. And always transform what you generate substantially.

**Option 3 — Pre-Made Bundles**
Creative Market, Design Bundles, Etsy digital assets — these are great for getting started fast. Just check the license: you need commercial use or POD-specific rights. Personal use? That's not enough.

## [TESTING & ITERATION — 6:00–7:00]

**[SCREEN: Meta Ads Manager interface with split test example]**

Here's how to test without blowing your budget.

**Step 1 — Small Engagement Ads**
Before running conversion ads, run $10–20 in engagement ads to 500–1,000 people in your target audience. This tells you if your design concept even attracts attention before you spend money trying to sell it.

**Step 2 — Split Test**
Run two designs simultaneously with identical targeting. Same product, same audience, same budget. Let it run for 3–5 days. Whichever gets better click-through rate, that's your winner.

**Step 3 — Know When to Kill It**
If a design gets zero traction after 2 weeks and multiple tests, move on. Don't fall in love with a design that isn't working. The POD game is about finding the designs that DO work and scaling those.

**When to iterate:** Solid concept, minor issues, specific feedback suggesting a clear fix.
**When to abandon:** No traction at all, legal issues, niche too narrow to sustain volume.

## [TYPOGRAPHY & COLOR PSYCHOLOGY — 7:00–7:45]

**[SCREEN: Font examples with dos and don'ts, color swatches with psychology notes]**

Quick hits on two force multipliers: typography and color.

**Typography rules:**
- Max 2 fonts per design
- Avoid basic built-in fonts — they scream "default"
- All-caps works for short phrases, not long copy
- Test at actual print size, not just on screen

**Color psychology by niche:**
- Black = edgy, power, streetwear
- White = minimal, clean, wellness
- Navy = trust, calm, professional
- Red = energy, urgency, fitness
- Green = nature, eco, calm
- Pink = playfulness, self-love, pride

**Pro tip:** The same design can look completely different on white vs. navy vs. black. Always mock up on your actual product color before you list.

## [FILE SPECS — 7:45–8:15]

**[SCREEN: Graphic showing file setup with annotations for bleed, safe zone, DPI]**

Non-negotiable file requirements for POD:

- **300 DPI minimum** (600 preferred)
- **CMYK color mode** — not RGB
- **AI or EPS format** for production (PNG only if specifically allowed)
- **Bleed area:** 0.125–0.25" on all sides
- **Safe zone:** Keep critical elements 0.125" inside trim
- **Convert all text to outlines** — never rely on embedded fonts
- **Download your POD partner's template** — Printful, Printify, Gelato all have different specs
